Irish (Gaeilge) is a Goidelic language of the Celtic branch of the Indo-European language family.

## Verb-Subject-Object Order
Irish follows a Verb-Subject-Object (VSO) word order, which differs from English (SVO).
Example: "Léann Máire an leabhar." (Máire reads the book.)
Literal: "Reads Máire the book."

## The Copula
Irish has two verbs meaning "to be": the copula (is) and the substantive verb (bí).
The copula (is) links a subject to a noun or adjective predicate.
Example: "Is múinteoir í." (She is a teacher.)
The substantive verb (bí) describes states, locations, and ongoing actions.
Example: "Tá sí sa bhaile." (She is at home.)

## Initial Mutations
Irish uses initial consonant mutations to mark grammatical relationships.
There are two main mutations: lenition (séimhiú) and eclipsis (urú).
Lenition adds 'h' after the initial consonant: "cat" → "chat".
Eclipsis prefixes a new consonant: "bord" → "mbord".

## Numbers
Cardinal numbers 1–10: aon, dó, trí, ceathair, cúig, sé, seacht, ocht, naoi, deich.
Numbers cause mutations on the following noun.
